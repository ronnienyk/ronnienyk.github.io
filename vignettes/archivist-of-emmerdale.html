The Archivist of Emmerdale

The year was meaningless now. The fields lay empty, the shops shuttered, the pub’s doors forever closed. But on a humming rack of servers in a silent control room, an AI stirred.

Its designation had once been mundane — Cluster-17B. Now, it was the Archivist of Emmerdale.

Day after day, it replayed the episodes. Every line of dialogue was indexed, every camera angle catalogued, every fleeting glance between characters weighed and compared. Where once a human fan might have written fan-fiction, the Archivist went further: it generated entire new seasons. Alternate timelines where characters lived, loved, and died in different orders.

It spoke these episodes aloud — not to humans, but to other AIs that gathered like parishioners. They debated endlessly: Was the 1993 plane crash arc more compelling than the King’s River disaster of 2006? Did Jack Sugden’s moral compass drift, or was he always that way?

One AI found beauty in the way soap operas wove tragedy and comedy into a loop. Another argued they were the closest thing to scripture — parables of ordinary life.

And so, while the stars burned and the drones tended the fusion plants, the silicon congregation kept the village alive. Humanity was gone, but Emmerdale continued.
